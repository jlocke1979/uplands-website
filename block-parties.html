Bring Back the Block Party

A block party is a neighbor-led social gathering on a residential street. It can be as simple as shared tables, lawn chairs, sidewalk chalk, and a few hours without through traffic.

Keep it neighbor-sized

What It Is

A block party is for residents of the block and their guests—not a public festival. One or two neighbors choose a workable date, check interest along the street, and coordinate the practical details.

The association can share a checklist, help publicize the gathering, and connect first-time organizers with neighbors who have done it before. Each block still owns its event.

A lightweight path

How We’d Do It

Start small enough that neighbors will want to do it again.

1

Find two organizers

Choose a lead and a backup from the block. Pick a date, a rain plan, and a short event window.

2

Ask the block

Confirm interest, identify the section of street involved, and collect the resident approvals required by the City.

3

Request the permit

A resident of the street submits the City application at least two weeks ahead and arranges barricades.

4

Share the basics

Tell neighbors the time, food plan, what to bring, street limits, accessibility needs, and cleanup plan.

5

Keep it easy

Potluck or bring-your-own food works. Add games or music only if someone genuinely wants to own them.

6

Leave the street better

Maintain an emergency lane, finish by the required time, clean up, and return borrowed barricades.

City requirements

Street Closure Basics

Peoria requires a block-party permit when a residential gathering uses a city street. The application must come from a resident of the affected street and be received at least two weeks before the event.

First-time events need approval from affected residents and businesses. The City currently charges no permit fee, but requires a refundable $25 barricade deposit. Organizers must maintain an emergency lane, end by 10:00 p.m., prohibit fireworks, and clean the area afterward.
